Defining Institutions: Why Modern Data Demands a New Empirical Institutional Theory


Abstract
The digital platforms that now mediate a substantial share of social and economic life present institutional theory with a defining paradox. On the one hand, platforms produce behavioral trace data of a granularity and scale unimaginable in the era of classical institutionalism-billions of timestamped reactions to informational signals. On the other hand, the foundational definitions of the discipline, particularly those inherited from Douglass North and W. Richard Scott, fail to travel to this data in any operationally coherent way. Drawing on a systematic review of the A/B testing architectures of Meta and Google, this study demonstrates that the conceptual apparatus of institutions-as-rules is not merely difficult to operationalize but is structurally incompatible with how real behavioral patterns emerge, stabilize, and dissolve on digital platforms. The paper documents three empirical fracture points: the algorithmic disruption of random assignment in platform experiments, the systematic exclusion of demographic groups from informational signals via opaque machine-learning optimization, and the platform-as-institutional-environment problem, wherein the signal generator itself is the primary architect of behavioral spectra. Anchored in Rapoport's subjectivism of system-identification, we propose that institutions must be reconceived as empirically distinguishable, statistically stable clusters of behavioral trajectories-carved from data by the analyst, not discovered as pre-existing entities. This redefinition provides the conceptual foundation for a full three-tier research program encompassing base definitions, a theoretical explanatory scaffold, and a suite of mathematical operationalization tools. 
Keywords: institutional theory, digital platforms, behavioral clusters, A/B testing, measurement crisis, Rapoport, subjectivism, operationalization, algorithmic governance

1. The Paradox at the Heart of Institutional Theory
There is an uncomfortable irony at the center of institutional theory. The discipline that set out to explain how collective behavior becomes stable, predictable, and self-reinforcing now finds itself unable to measure its own central object. Across three decades of cumulative empirical work, institutional scholars have relied on a conceptual vocabulary—rules, norms, constraints, shared mental models—that resists every attempt at direct operationalization. The definitions that structure the field are not wrong in spirit. They are wrong in kind: they describe entities that cannot, even in principle, be observed in the behavioral trace data that modern platforms actually produce.
This is not a minor methodological inconvenience. It is a crisis that has been building in plain sight. Hodgson (2015) called it directly: the "rules-versus-equilibria" debate that has consumed institutional economics for decades is a proxy war over the fact that institutions are, by one definition, "systems of rules that structure social interactions," while equilibria are the regularities that arise from them—and confusing the two has produced an empirical dead end. Voigt (2018) was blunter still: "Defining institutions in a way that they can be operationalized is not a trivial task" (p. 10), and the measurement instruments on offer, from the Worldwide Governance Indicators to the Economic Freedom of the World Index, capture policy outcomes rather than institutional patterns themselves. The gap between what institutional theory claims to study and what it can actually measure is not narrowing. It is widening.
Meanwhile, a radically different empirical reality has emerged. Digital platforms—Meta, Google, TikTok, Amazon, Stripe—now mediate a substantial share of social and economic life. These platforms do not merely host institutions; they produce them, in real time, at scale, with a transparency of behavioral data that classical institutionalism never anticipated. Every click, every scroll, every purchase, every skipped notification is a timestamped, ID-tagged reaction to an identifiable informational signal. The data exist in abundance. The conceptual tools to interpret them remain anchored to latent constructs that cannot be mapped onto these data.
This paper argues that the failure is not technical but structural. Classical institutional theory defines institutions in terms of latent constructs—rules, norms, shared beliefs—that are fundamentally unobservable in the behavioral trace data that modern platforms generate. The remedy is not better measurement instruments for old definitions. It is a new definition altogether: institutions as empirically distinguishable, statistically stable clusters of behavioral trajectories, carved from data by the analyst rather than discovered as pre-existing entities. This reorientation does not merely update institutional theory. It reconstitutes its analytical object from the ground up.
2. What Classical Theory Actually Says (And What It Cannot Measure)
To understand why the measurement gap exists, we need to see exactly what the dominant definitions claim and what they leave unobservable. Two formulations have structured the field for over three decades.
2.1 North's Rules of the Game
Douglass North's definition, first articulated in Institutions, Institutional Change and Economic Performance (1990), is the most widely cited in economics and political science. "Institutions are the rules of the game in a society," North wrote, "or, more formally, are the humanly devised constraints that shape human interaction" (p. 3). The power of this formulation lies in its breadth: it encompasses formal rules (laws, constitutions, contracts) and informal constraints (traditions, codes of conduct, cultural expectations). It also introduced the critical distinction between institutions and organizations—institutions are the rules; organizations are the players.
But the metaphor carries a hidden cost. By defining institutions as constraints, North embedded a game-theoretic assumption: that actors know the rules and adjust their behavior accordingly. This assumption is untestable in any direct sense. Rules, as North himself acknowledged, exist partly as mental constructs—"the incentive structure" that guides choice. If an institution is a constraint no one is aware of, or a norm that no one follows, has it been measured? Or has the theorist merely asserted its existence to explain an observed outcome after the fact?
Krul (2018), in a comprehensive critical reconstruction of North's intellectual trajectory, showed that the "rules of the game" metaphor is rooted in an implicit game-theoretic model of society that fails to specify how actors come to know the rules. The identification problem is foundational: if you cannot specify who learns what rule, when, and through what mechanism, the entire empirical apparatus rests on inference rather than observation. Coase's (1937) early work on the nature of the firm provides a complementary starting point: if the boundaries of organizations are themselves determined by transaction costs, then the "rules" governing institutional behavior may be less like fixed parameters and more like equilibrium outcomes of an underlying cost structure—an interpretation that only deepens the operationalization problem.
2.2 Scott's Three Pillars
W. Richard Scott's alternative framework, developed over three editions of Institutions and Organizations (2014), widened the lens. Institutions, Scott argued, comprise three pillars: regulative (formal rules and sanctions), normative (shared expectations and obligations), and cultural-cognitive (shared frames of meaning and taken-for-granted assumptions). This tripartite structure captured something North's model missed: that institutions operate not only through explicit incentives but through the background assumptions that make certain behaviors seem natural, obvious, or unthinkable.
The limitation, however, is the same one. The cultural-cognitive pillar in particular resists operationalization. How does one measure a "taken-for-granted assumption"? Scott himself acknowledged that the three pillars are not always empirically distinguishable and that their relative importance varies across contexts. The framework provides a vocabulary for describing institutions after they have been identified; it does not provide a procedure for identifying them in the first place.
Aoki (2007) attempted a synthesis, arguing that institutions are "self-sustaining, salient patterns of social interaction represented by the shared beliefs of the players" (p. 5). This was a genuine advance—it shifted the ontological status of institutions from external constraints to emergent equilibria—but it relocated the measurement problem rather than solving it. Now the challenge was to measure shared beliefs, which are no more directly observable than rules. Weik and Walgenbach (2021) have since proposed a latent-variable measurement model for institutional constructs, offering a formal test of whether these latent structures can be recovered from observable data—their results suggest they cannot, at least not without the kind of reorientation this paper proposes. Hindriks (2022) provides a recent synthesis of the rules-versus-equilibria debate that further confirms the shared measurement problem on both sides.
2.3 The Measurement Dead End
The cumulative effect of three decades of definitional refinement is a field that can describe institutions eloquently and measure them barely at all. To say that an institution "constrains" or "enables" behavior, while simultaneously defining the institution as the constraint itself, produces a conceptual circularity that resists falsification. If institutions are rules and norms, and rules and norms are inferred from the behavior that they are supposed to explain, then institutional theory risks becoming an elaborate tautology.
The practical consequence is visible in the tools the field relies on. The Worldwide Governance Indicators aggregate perceptions of governance quality across countries; they do not measure institutions directly. The Economic Freedom of the World Index scores policy environments; it does not identify the behavioral patterns that constitute institutional life. These instruments are useful for their stated purposes, but they measure the outputs of institutions, not the institutions themselves. The mediating step—how a rule becomes a pattern, how a norm becomes a routine—remains a black box.
This is the measurement crisis. And it is not a problem that can be solved within the existing framework, because the framework's foundational terms are not merely hard to measure—they are structurally non-operationalizable. They describe properties that, by definition, exist beneath or behind observable behavior. No amount of better data collection can make "the rule behind the behavior" visible if the rule is, by the theory's own account, an inference from the behavior.
3. Digital Platforms as Empirical Stress Tests
If classical institutional theory cannot be operationalized, it should at least be able to account for the clearest empirical cases available. Digital platforms provide exactly that: environments where behavioral data exist at unprecedented granularity, where the informational signals that drive behavior are identifiable and timestamped, and where institutional patterns emerge, stabilize, and dissolve on timescales of days rather than decades. If institutional theory works, it should work here. The evidence suggests otherwise.
3.1 The A/B Testing Illusion: When Random Assignment Collapses
The most direct empirical window into the measurement gap is provided by the A/B testing infrastructure that underlies modern platform operations. A systematic review by Cornil and Hardisty (2025), published in the International Journal of Research in Marketing, examined all known peer-reviewed studies that utilized the A/B testing tools of Meta (Facebook) and Google. The findings are analytically devastating for any theory of institutions that assumes a stable, rule-based environment.
The core methodological problem is straightforward: platform A/B tests systematically violate the random assignment principle that experimental science depends on. When an advertiser delivers two variants of an advertisement, the platform's machine-learning algorithms actively select which users see which variant, optimizing for predicted click-through probability rather than random allocation. As Cornil and Hardisty explained in their public summary: "there is no real random assignment… even if we asked people at Facebook, 'Why was this group of people selected?', they wouldn't know the answer" (as reported in EurekAlert, 2025). Every Facebook user is an unwitting participant in approximately ten simultaneous experiments at any given moment.
Goli et al. (2022) formalize this problem, documenting interference bias in platform ranking experiments and showing that the assumption of independent treatment effects—foundational to classical experimental design—is systematically violated in algorithmic environments. Braun and Schwartz (2022) extend this critique to advertising contexts specifically, demonstrating how algorithmic targeting confounds A/B test results in ways that make measured treatment effects unreliable as indicators of true causal impact. Wu et al. (2023) add a further complication: even when experiments are properly designed, non-stationarities in platform metrics introduce bias over time, meaning that the behavioral data collected through A/B testing are not the stable measurements they appear to be.
From an institutional theory perspective, this is not a methodological footnote. It means that the "signal" (the ad creative, the interface element, the policy notification) and the "filter" (the algorithm that determines who is exposed to it) are not independent variables. The institutional environment of the platform is not a neutral medium for the governance of behavior—it is an active, opaque agent in the production of behavioral outcomes. The implication is stark: any institutional analysis of platform-mediated behavior must account for a signal source that is itself adaptive, non-neutral, and partially unobservable. Neither North's "rules of the game" metaphor nor Scott's "cultural-cognitive pillar" offers any analytical purchase on this condition, because both assume that the informational environment is a stable backdrop against which agents act, not a dynamic, self-modifying system.
What makes this finding particularly damaging is its scale. If institutional theory cannot account for the experimental conditions under which billions of behavioral observations are generated, the scope of its explanatory claims must be radically circumscribed. The data exist in abundance. The conceptual tools to interpret them do not.
3.2 Algorithmic Exclusion: Institutions No One Chose
A second fracture point concerns the systematic exclusion of demographic groups from informational signals. The Cornil and Hardisty (2025) review documents well-established evidence that platform algorithms, optimizing for advertising cost efficiency, can unintentionally exclude entire categories of users from important informational signals. The foundational empirical study here is Lambrecht and Tucker (2018), who demonstrated that women are significantly less likely to be targeted by ads for STEM (science, technology, engineering, and mathematics) education—not because of any explicit policy, but purely because of algorithmic optimization. The underlying mechanism is coldly economic: women are more expensive to target on social media platforms, and the algorithm maximizes the number of clicks per dollar by preferentially delivering STEM ads to men.
Mehrabi et al. (2022) situate this finding within a broader landscape of bias and fairness in machine learning, documenting how algorithmic systems systematically encode and amplify social inequalities through mechanisms that are invisible to both users and regulators. The UBC Sauder School of Business (2025) news release, which brought the STEM-ad finding to wider public attention, underscores the real-world stakes: an entire demographic category was being structurally excluded from exposure to educational opportunity by an optimization process that no human designed, no rule authorized, and no oversight mechanism detected.
For institutional theory, this finding has consequences that go far beyond advertising ethics. Since the classical framework defines institutions by the normative and regulative frameworks that constrain behavior, it lacks the conceptual vocabulary to account for a situation in which the structure of behavioral options is being determined by an algorithmic process that no human intended and no rule specified. No formal rule states that women should be excluded from STEM information. No cultural-cognitive norm prescribes the exclusion. No enforcement mechanism sanctions it. And yet, a stable, reproducible behavioral outcome—a gender-differentiated information environment—is produced with high statistical reliability.
This is an institution generated by an algorithmic filter, not by rule-following behavior. It is a behavioral pattern that meets every functional criterion of institutional persistence (it is stable, it shapes action, it is self-reinforcing) while lacking every feature that classical theory identifies as constitutive of an institution (no rule, no norm, no shared belief). If institutional theory cannot accommodate this phenomenon without stretching its definitions beyond recognition, the definitions themselves—not the phenomenon—must be the object of revision.
3.3 The Platform-as-Architect Problem
The third fracture point arises from the unique structural position of digital platforms in the institutional field. Platforms are simultaneously: (a) generators of informational signals, (b) architects of the filters that determine how signals are received, (c) the owners of the data infrastructure that records behavioral trajectories, and (d) the governed space within which institutional patterns of user behavior emerge. This conflation of roles has no analogue in the classical institutionalist framework.
In North's model, institutions exist "above" the actors they constrain—they are the rules of the game, not one of the players. In Scott's framework, institutions are environmental conditions that organizations inhabit. In both cases, there is a clean separation between the institution and the entities it governs. A digital platform destroys this separation. It writes the rules (through its terms of service and interface design), enforces them (through algorithmic filtering and content moderation), records compliance and deviation (through behavioral logging), and adjusts the rules in real time (through A/B testing and machine-learning updates). The platform is the legislator, the police, the court, and the laboratory.
Gillespie (2018) laid essential groundwork for this analysis in his examination of platforms as custodians who shape public discourse through hidden editorial decisions. His earlier work (Gillespie, 2010) on "the politics of platforms" argued that platforms function as architectural infrastructures that embed political choices in their design—a thesis that has only become more relevant as platforms have evolved from passive hosting services to active governors of behavioral ecosystems. Matias and Mou (2018), in foundational work on platform-scale behavioral experiments, demonstrated how communities develop complex institutional governance logics—"committees, boards, juries, and even more complex institutional logics"—entirely within virtual environments where the rules are not predetermined but emerge through interaction.
White (2025) has recently proposed a conceptual framework for understanding technology corporations as de facto regulators, theorizing the mechanisms through which platforms exercise institutional authority without formal mandates. Just and Latzer (2017) argue that algorithmic selection itself functions as a form of institutional governance, constructing social reality in ways that parallel classical institutions but with critical differences in transparency, accountability, and reversibility. Doshi and Schmidt (2024) explore how transparency-based mechanisms can enable governance to emerge on platforms without formal rules—directly relevant to the "institutions no one chose" problem. The very fact that platforms can run large-scale experiments on governance logic represents a historically unprecedented capacity for institutional engineering, one that requires rethinking the very object of institutional analysis.
The platform-as-architect problem is not a theoretical edge case. It is the dominant empirical condition of institutional life for billions of people. Any framework that treats institutions as stable, exogenous constraints cannot account for environments in which the constraints themselves are fluid, opaque, and experimentally manipulated on a continuous basis.
3.4 Bridging Example: The Behavioral Cluster Analysis of Algorithmic Exclusion
Before introducing the full framework, it is worth illustrating how the proposed redefinition would handle one of the three fracture points in concrete analytical terms. Consider the gender-differentiated STEM-ad finding documented in §3.2.
Under classical institutional theory, one would need to identify the rule or norm that produces the observed pattern: women see fewer STEM ads. But no such rule exists. There is no regulation, no cultural norm, no shared belief that mandates this exclusion. The pattern is real and stable, but it cannot be explained by any mechanism that classical institutional theory recognizes.
Under the behavioral cluster framework proposed here, the analysis proceeds differently. First, we identify the informational signal: the STEM-education advertisement, with its associated targeting parameters and bid structure. Second, we map the reaction spectrum: the full distribution of user responses to this signal across the platform's population, disaggregated by demographic category. Third, we identify the behavioral clusters: statistically stable groupings of reaction trajectories that emerge from the interaction of the signal with the platform's algorithmic filter. The result is two (or more) distinct clusters—one dominated by male users who receive and respond to STEM ads at high rates, and one dominated by female users who are largely excluded from exposure.
The key insight is that the cluster framework does not need to posit a rule or norm to explain the pattern. The pattern is the institution. It is a stable, self-reinforcing behavioral regularity produced by the interaction of an informational signal with an algorithmic selection mechanism. The framework can measure it (through clustering algorithms applied to reaction trajectories), track its stability over time (through stationarity tests), and compare it across platforms or policy interventions (through inter-cluster transition matrices). In doing so, it provides exactly the kind of empirical grip on the phenomenon that classical institutional theory cannot supply.
4. A New Empirical Foundation
The three fracture points converge on a single conclusion: classical institutional theory is not poorly operationalized. It is structurally non-operationalizable, because it defines institutions in terms of latent properties (rules, norms, shared mental models) rather than manifest properties (observed, stable, and clustered patterns of behavior in response to identifiable informational signals). The remedy requires not a new measurement technique but a fundamental reorientation of the analytical object itself.
4.1 From Rules to Behavioral Clusters
The core proposal is this: institutions should be defined as empirically distinguishable, statistically stable clusters of behavioral trajectories that arise from a spectrum of possible reactions to an identifiable informational signal. Before proceeding, it is essential to define the three primitives of this framework precisely.
A behavioral trajectory is the time-ordered sequence of reactions produced by a single agent in response to a stream of identifiable informational signals. It is the individual-level analogue of what classical theory calls "behavior"—but unlike the classical concept, it is fully specified by timestamped, observable actions (or inactions) recorded in digital trace data.
A behavioral cluster is a grouping of similar behavioral trajectories identified through unsupervised learning techniques. Clusters are not assumed; they are constructed by the analyst using transparent, reproducible criteria (e.g., a minimum divergence threshold between behavioral distributions in adjacent time windows). A cluster is what the analyst identifies as sufficiently stable and distinct to warrant the label "institution."
A behavioral spectrum is the full distribution of possible reactions to a given informational signal within a defined population at a given time. It captures the range of behavioral variation before clustering, and it serves as the baseline against which the stability and distinctiveness of clusters can be assessed.
This triad of concepts replaces the classical vocabulary of rules, norms, and shared beliefs with constructs that map directly onto the data that digital platforms generate. The shift is more radical than it first appears. It does not merely replace the word "rule" with the word "cluster." It changes the epistemological status of the analytical object. Under the classical framework, an institution is something inferred from behavior—a latent cause that is assumed to stand behind the regularity. Under the proposed framework, an institution is something directly identified in the behavior—a pattern that is clustered, bounded, and tracked without reference to any unobservable mediating construct.
Consider a concrete example. Under classical institutional theory, "tax compliance" is an institution because a rule (the tax code) constrains behavior. Under the new framework, "tax compliance" is a stable behavioral cluster—a set of individual trajectories that converge on the same observable pattern (filing returns, reporting income, making payments) in response to an identifiable signal (the tax code, enforcement communications, social expectations). The cluster is identified by its statistical properties, not by the rule that supposedly causes it. Whether a rule exists, and whether anyone believes in it, are separate empirical questions that may or may not be relevant to the cluster's persistence.
This distinction matters because it dissolves the circularity that has plagued institutional measurement. If an institution is a behavioral cluster, then measuring it requires the same tools used to measure any statistical regularity: clustering algorithms, stationarity tests, transition matrices. The measurement does not depend on anyone's beliefs, intentions, or interpretations. It depends only on the data.
4.2 Rapoport's Subjectivism as Methodological Framework
The philosophical foundation for this redefinition is drawn from an unexpected source: the systems science of Anatol Rapoport. In a seminal contribution to general systems theory, Rapoport (1970) argued that any system—and, by extension, any institution—is not discovered as a given entity but constructed by the analyst who decides, for specific research purposes, what to carve out of the whole and how to define its boundaries.
This subjectivism directly destabilizes the essentialism inherent in North's and Scott's definitions. There is no "real" institution waiting to be isolated, no ontological bedrock beneath the patterns we observe. Instead, an institution is whatever the analyst, using transparent and reproducible criteria, identifies as a sufficiently stable and distinct behavioral cluster. The criteria are not given by nature; they are chosen by the researcher. And that choice—what threshold of stability to require, what clustering algorithm to use, what time window to observe—is a methodological decision, not an ontological discovery.
This does not make institutional analysis arbitrary. It makes it honest. The analyst's choices become the explicit, testable claims of the research rather than hidden assumptions smuggled in through definitions. If a researcher defines stability as a divergence of less than a given threshold between behavioral distributions in adjacent time windows, then exactly what counts as an institution is determined by that threshold-and anyone can challenge, replicate, or refine the choice. Operationalization becomes not a downstream methodological afterthought but the primary conceptual act. For readers unfamiliar with the 1970 special issue, Rapoport's later treatment in General System Theory: Essential Concepts & Applications (1986) provides a more accessible entry point to these ideas.
Zuboff's (2022) macro-level institutional analysis of surveillance capitalism offers a compelling convergence point. Her SSRN paper theorizes the "death match" between institutional orders in the information age, showing how behavioral extraction at scale produces new institutional configurations that fit uneasily within classical frameworks—precisely the kind of phenomenon the cluster-based approach is designed to capture.
4.3 The Three-Tier Research Architecture
The redefinition of institutions as behavioral clusters supports a full research architecture organized into three tiers.
Tier 1: Base Definitions. The foundational layer consists of primitives that are directly translatable into digital behavioral traces. An informational signal is any objectively recordable change in the information environment-a timestamped event with a source and a type. A reaction is a discrete, logged action or inaction. A behavioral cluster is a grouping of similar reaction trajectories identified through unsupervised learning. Each primitive maps onto a specific type of data that platforms already collect. No unobservable constructs are required.
Tier 2: Theoretical Scaffold. The second tier provides explanatory mechanisms connecting the primitives. These include perception filters (the agent-specific transformation of a signal into a subjective stimulus), habituation and social learning as micro-foundations of pattern stability, opportunism as the behavioral premise (agents interpret signals through a hierarchy of personal-to-societal interests, following Williamson, 1985), and signal-to-norm dynamics in which institutional patterns, once stabilized, feed back to reshape the informational environment through which new signals are processed.
Trumble (2025) provides independent, convergent support for this tier by proposing that platform metrics function as "gravitational centers of behavior," producing stable behavioral patterns that are directly measurable-an argument that emerges from a different theoretical tradition but arrives at a compatible conclusion about the observability of institutional phenomena. Entsminger and Westgren (2019) demonstrate methodologically how cluster analysis can operationalize an institutional taxonomy using observational data, providing a precedent for the Tier 3 toolkit applied to organizational forms.
Tier 3: Mathematical Operationalization. The third tier provides a computational and statistical toolkit for making the framework empirically verifiable. This includes trajectory clustering (UMAP, HDBSCAN), stationarity testing (Augmented Dickey-Fuller, KPSS), inter-cluster transition matrices (Markov chains for measuring institutional fluidity and competition), and network-based contagion models for simulating social propagation and cascade dynamics. Taken together, these tools constitute a complete measurement system: from raw behavioral logs to identified institutions, with quantified uncertainty at every step.
The three tiers are not sequential stages of a single pipeline. They are interdependent layers of analysis. The primitives (Tier 1) constrain what the theory can explain; the scaffold (Tier 2) determines what questions are worth asking; and the operationalization tools (Tier 3) determine what can be empirically adjudicated. Progress in any tier creates new possibilities-and new obligations-in the others.
5. Why This Matters Beyond the Academy
The redefinition of institutions as behavioral clusters is not only an academic exercise. It has immediate, practical implications for anyone who designs, regulates, or competes with digital platforms.
For product managers and founders, the framework provides a precise language for describing what their products actually do. A product does not "create a habit" in some vague motivational sense. It generates a statistically stable behavioral cluster in response to a specific informational signal (a notification, a prompt, a default setting). The framework's measurement apparatus-reaction classification, stability testing, spectrum analysis-can be applied directly to product telemetry data to assess whether a new feature has created a genuine institution or merely a transient spike in engagement.
For regulators and policymakers, the framework offers an alternative to the blunt instruments of content moderation and market regulation. If an algorithmic system is producing stable behavioral clusters that no one intended-such as the gender-differentiated information environment documented by Lambrecht and Tucker (2018)-the framework provides the diagnostic vocabulary to identify, measure, and address the problem. Rather than asking "is this platform fair?" (a normative question with no operational answer), regulators can ask "what behavioral clusters has this platform stabilized, and do they match the intended outcomes of the signals it generates?" (an empirical question with a clear measurement strategy).
It is important to acknowledge that this framework has not yet been applied in a regulatory setting. The claims about what regulators "can ask" are empirical hypotheses grounded in the framework's theoretical properties, not demonstrated outcomes. Pilot studies-ideally in collaboration with platform governance teams or independent research organizations-will be necessary to validate the framework's practical utility in regulatory contexts. Marisetty et al. (2025) offer an encouraging early example: their analysis of India's UPI ecosystem demonstrates how digital public infrastructure can reshape institutional participation in measurable ways, suggesting that the cluster framework can indeed be applied to policy-relevant settings. Similarly, Leippold (2025) provides empirical evidence of platform governance mechanisms-specifically, Reddit's Community Notes system-in which platforms adjust institutional parameters in real time in response to user behavior, illustrating the "signal-to-norm dynamics" described in Tier 2.
For behavioral scientists and economists, the framework resolves a long-standing tension between laboratory and field research. The controlled experiment (a signal, a population, a measured response) and the observational study (a platform, a behavioral trace, a statistical pattern) are no longer incommensurable. They are applications of the same measurement framework at different scales and with different degrees of experimental control.
For technology companies, the framework introduces a new form of accountability. If institutions are behavioral clusters, and platforms are the architects of the signals that produce them, then platforms bear empirical responsibility for the spectra they generate. This is not a moral argument-it is a methodological one. The data to assess institutional impact are already being collected, every second, on every major platform. The question is whether the analytical framework exists to interpret them.
6. Conclusion
The measurement crisis in institutional theory is not a gap that can be closed by incremental refinement of existing concepts. The foundational definitions-institutions as rules, norms, constraints-are structurally incompatible with the kind of data that digital platforms now produce in abundance. The constructs are too abstract to be operationalized, too latent to be tested, and too entangled with the behavior they are meant to explain to support falsifiable claims.
This paper has demonstrated the failure through three empirical fracture points: the collapse of random assignment in platform A/B tests, the algorithmic exclusion of demographic groups from informational signals, and the structural impossibility of separating the institutional environment from the entities that architect it. Each fracture point reveals the same underlying problem: the classical framework assumes a stable, transparent, human-readable institutional environment, while the empirical reality is dynamic, opaque, and machine-generated.
The proposed alternative-institutions as statistically stable behavioral clusters, identified by the analyst through transparent criteria-does not discard the insights of classical institutional theory. It preserves the core observation that collective behavior exhibits stable, self-reinforcing patterns. It simply relocates the analytical object from the unobservable (rules, norms, beliefs) to the observable (behavioral trajectories, statistical distributions, cluster stability). In doing so, it transforms institutional analysis from an interpretative enterprise into an empirical science-one that can leverage the unprecedented data resources of the digital age.
The research program outlined here is ambitious in scope but minimal in its assumptions. It starts with observable behavior. It uses statistical tools to identify stability. It treats the analyst's criteria as explicit, testable choices rather than hidden ontological commitments. And it opens the door to a cumulative, replicable science of institutions-one that can finally answer the question that has defined the field since its inception: not what institutions should be, but what they are, empirically, in the data.
