# Rules vs Equilibria Debate

## Summary

The **rules vs equilibria debate** is a central theoretical division in institutional theory about whether institutions are best understood as:
- **Rules**: Prescriptive constraints on behavior (North's approach)
- **Equilibria**: Stable outcomes of strategic interaction (game-theoretic approach)

## The Rules Position

**Proponents**: Douglass North, W. Richard Scott

**Core argument**: Institutions are humanly devised rules, norms, and constraints that shape human interaction. They exist prior to and independent of specific interactions.

**Key claims**:
- Institutions can be identified and catalogued
- Institutional change involves rule modification
- Rules explain behavioral regularities

## The Equilibria Position

**Proponents**: Avner Greif, game theorists

**Core argument**: Institutions are equilibrium outcomes of repeated games. They emerge from strategic interaction rather than preceding it.

**Key claims**:
- Institutions are self-enforcing patterns of behavior
- No external enforcement needed
- Explains institutional persistence without invoking "rules"

## Why This Debate Matters

The debate is **unresolvable under current definitions** because:
1. Neither position offers operationalizable measurement criteria
2. Both fail on digital platforms where behavior doesn't map to either framework
3. The distinction itself may be a false dichotomy

## Resolution Through Behavioral Clusters

The [[New Empirical Foundation]] proposes moving beyond this debate by:
- Focusing on observable behavioral patterns, not theoretical categories
- Using clustering methods to identify institutional structures empirically
- Treating "rules" and "equilibria" as different descriptions of the same underlying behavioral clusters
