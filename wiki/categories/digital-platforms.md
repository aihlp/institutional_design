# Category: Digital Platforms as Empirical Stress Tests

## Overview

This category documents how digital platforms expose fundamental fractures in classical institutional theory through empirical phenomena that the classical framework cannot explain.

## Why Platforms Are Stress Tests

Digital platforms provide unprecedented empirical conditions:
- **Granular behavioral data**: Every click, scroll, purchase logged
- **Identifiable signals**: Timestamped, sourced informational events
- **Real-time institution production**: Patterns emerge, stabilize, dissolve rapidly
- **Algorithmic mediation**: Behavior shaped by opaque ML systems

If institutional theory works, it should work here. The evidence suggests otherwise.

## Three Fracture Points

### 1. [[A/B Testing Illusion]]
Platform A/B tests systematically violate random assignment:
- ML algorithms select users based on predicted behavior
- Signal and filter are entangled, not independent
- Neither North nor Scott can account for adaptive, opaque environments

### 2. [[Algorithmic Exclusion]]
Demographic groups excluded from signals without rules or norms:
- Women see fewer STEM ads due to cost optimization, not policy
- No rule, norm, or shared belief produces the pattern
- Yet stable, reproducible behavioral outcome emerges

### 3. [[Platform-as-Architect Problem]]
Platforms conflate roles that classical theory keeps separate:
- Legislator + Police + Court + Laboratory
- No separation between institution and governed entity
- Constraints are fluid, opaque, experimentally manipulated

## Implications

These fracture points demonstrate:
1. Classical theory assumes stable, transparent environments—reality is dynamic, opaque
2. Institutions can exist without rules, norms, or shared beliefs
3. The measurement gap is widening, not narrowing
4. A fundamental reorientation is required, not incremental refinement
