# Rapoport's Subjectivism

## Summary

**Anatol Rapoport** (1970, 1986) argued that any system—and by extension, any institution—is not discovered as a given entity but **constructed by the analyst** who decides what to carve out of the whole and how to define its boundaries.

## Core Thesis

There is no "real" institution waiting to be isolated, no ontological bedrock beneath the patterns we observe. Instead:

> **An institution is whatever the analyst, using transparent and reproducible criteria, identifies as a sufficiently stable and distinct behavioral cluster.**

## Destabilizing Essentialism

This subjectivism directly challenges the essentialism in classical definitions:

| North/Scott Assumption | Rapoportian Alternative |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| Institution exists independently of observer | Institution constructed by analytical act |
| Definitions discover natural kinds | Definitions create analytical categories |
| Ontological question ("What IS an institution?") | Methodological question ("How shall we identify institutions?") |
| Hidden assumptions in definitions | Explicit choices in criteria |

## The Analyst's Choices

The following are **methodological decisions**, not ontological discoveries:

1. **What threshold of stability to require**
   - How much temporal consistency is "enough"?
   - Statistical criterion, not natural fact

2. **What clustering algorithm to use**
   - UMAP, HDBSCAN, k-means, etc.
   - Each produces different boundaries

3. **What time window to observe**
   - Days? Months? Years?
   - Determines what counts as "stable"

## Making Institutional Analysis Honest

This does **not** make institutional analysis arbitrary. It makes it **honest**:

### Before (Classical Framework)
- Analyst's choices = hidden assumptions smuggled through definitions
- Operationalization = downstream methodological afterthought
- Disagreements = unresolvable ontological debates

### After (Rapoportian Framework)
- Analyst's choices = explicit, testable claims of the research
- Operationalization = **primary conceptual act**
- Disagreements = empirical questions about which criteria produce useful results

## Example: Defining Stability

A researcher might specify:
> "An institution is a behavioral cluster where divergence between behavioral distributions in adjacent time windows remains below 0.15 (measured by Jensen-Shannon divergence) over a minimum of 30 consecutive days."

**Implications**:
- Exactly what counts as an institution is determined by that threshold
- Anyone can challenge the choice (too strict? too lenient?)
- Anyone can replicate using same criterion
- Anyone can refine with different threshold and compare results

## Sources

### Primary
- **Rapoport (1970)**: Seminal contribution to general systems theory
- **Rapoport (1986)**: *General System Theory: Essential Concepts & Applications* - more accessible entry point

### Convergent Support
- **Zuboff (2022)**: Macro-level institutional analysis of surveillance capitalism
  - Theorizes "death match" between institutional orders in information age
  - Behavioral extraction at scale produces new institutional configurations
  - Fits uneasily within classical frameworks
  - Precisely the kind of phenomenon cluster-based approach captures

## Why This Matters for the Behavioral Cluster Framework

The Rapoportian foundation:
1. **Legitimates** the analyst's role in constructing institutions
2. **Requires** transparency about criteria and thresholds
3. **Enables** cumulative science through reproducibility
4. **Dissolves** ontological debates into methodological choices
