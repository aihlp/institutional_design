# Platform-as-Architect Problem

## Summary

Digital platforms occupy a unique structural position that destroys the classical separation between institutions and the entities they govern.

## The Four Conflated Roles

Platforms are simultaneously:

| Role | Function | Classical Analogue |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| **(a) Signal Generator** | Produces informational signals | N/A - no analogue |
| **(b) Filter Architect** | Designs how signals are received | N/A - no analogue |
| **(c) Data Owner** | Records behavioral trajectories | N/A - no analogue |
| **(d) Governed Space** | Hosts institutional patterns | The "field" or "environment" |

**Critical insight**: This conflation has **no analogue** in classical institutionalist framework.

## Classical Separation vs. Platform Reality

### North's Model
- Institutions exist **"above"** actors they constrain
- Rules of the game, not one of the players
- Clean separation between institution and governed entities

### Scott's Framework
- Institutions are **environmental conditions** organizations inhabit
- External to the organizations they shape
- Clear boundary between institution and actor

### Platform Reality
- **Destroys this separation entirely**
- Platform writes rules (terms of service, interface design)
- Platform enforces rules (algorithmic filtering, content moderation)
- Platform records compliance/deviation (behavioral logging)
- Platform adjusts rules in real time (A/B testing, ML updates)

**The platform is**: Legislator + Police + Court + Laboratory

## Foundational Scholarship

### Gillespie (2018)
- Platforms as custodians shaping public discourse
- Hidden editorial decisions structure what users see
- *Custodians of the Internet*

### Gillespie (2010)
- "The Politics of Platforms"
- Platforms function as architectural infrastructures
- Embed political choices in design
- Thesis increasingly relevant as platforms evolved

### Matias and Mou (2018)
- Platform-scale behavioral experiments
- Communities develop complex institutional governance logics
- "Committees, boards, juries, and even more complex institutional logics"
- Rules emerge through interaction, not predetermined

### White (2025)
- Technology corporations as **de facto regulators**
- Mechanisms of institutional authority without formal mandates
- Conceptual framework for platform governance

### Just and Latzer (2017)
- Algorithmic selection as **institutional governance**
- Constructs social reality parallel to classical institutions
- Critical differences: transparency, accountability, reversibility

### Doshi and Schmidt (2024)
- Transparency-based mechanisms enable governance without formal rules
- Directly relevant to "institutions no one chose" problem
- Historically unprecedented capacity for institutional engineering

## Why This Matters

The platform-as-architect problem is **not a theoretical edge case**:
- Dominant empirical condition for billions of people
- Any framework treating institutions as stable, exogenous constraints fails
- Constraints themselves are:
  - Fluid (change continuously)
  - Opaque (not visible to those governed)
  - Experimentally manipulated (A/B tested, ML-updated)

## Implications for Institutional Theory

Classical theory must account for environments where:
1. The rule-maker is also a player
2. Rules change faster than they can be studied
3. Enforcement is automated and opaque
4. Compliance data feeds back into rule modification
5. No separation exists between institution and architect
